# User Needs Discovery (Minimal)

## Purpose
Find out what people actually struggle with by asking about their past, not their
future — because everyone says yes to a hypothetical and almost nobody buys it.

## Core Techniques

### 1. Ask About Last Time, Not Next Time
"Would you use a tool that does X?" is worthless. It costs the respondent nothing
to say yes, and agreeing is the polite default. Replace intent questions with
recall questions about a specific, dated event.

| ❌ Bad | ✅ Good | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "Would you pay for automated invoice reconciliation?" | "Walk me through the last time you reconciled invoices. What day was it?" | Removes hypothetical agreement |
| "Do you find reporting difficult?" | "When did you last build a report? What made it take as long as it did?" | Difficulty is relative; the story is not |
| "How often do you export data?" | "How many times did you export data last week?" | Bounded recall beats self-estimated frequency |
| "Would you like this faster?" | "What did you do while it was running?" | Nobody says no to faster |
| "Is our onboarding confusing?" | "Where did you get stuck the first week? Show me." | Doesn't hand them the answer |

The core principle: talk about their life, not your idea; ask about specifics in
the past, not opinions about the future; talk less and listen more.

### 2. Chase the Workaround, Not the Complaint
Complaints are cheap. Effort is evidence. When someone has built a spreadsheet, a
Zapier chain, a shared doc, or hired a contractor to cover a gap, they have paid
real money or hours — that is a need with a price tag already attached.

Follow-ups that surface it:
- "What are you doing about that today?"
- "Who else touches that spreadsheet?"
- "What happens if you just don't do it that week?"
- "Have you tried to fix this before? What happened?"

If the answer to the last one is "no, never" the pain is real but not urgent.

### 3. Separate Stated Preference From Revealed Behaviour
Stated: what they tell you they want, value, or would do.
Revealed: what their calendar, bank statement, and clickstream say they did.

When the two conflict, revealed wins every time. Users say they want more
configuration options and then never leave the defaults. They say price is the
deciding factor and then buy the expensive one with better support. Treat
interview output as a source of hypotheses, and behavioural data as the test.

Ask for revealed signals inside the interview: "Can you open the tool and show
me?" A screen share of their real workflow is worth an hour of description.

### 4. Frame Findings as Job Stories
A job story ties a situation to a motivation and an expected outcome, and unlike
a persona it does not invent demographics you never measured.

> When **the month-end close lands and three systems disagree on revenue**, I want
> **to see which records differ and why, without exporting to Excel**, so I can
> **sign off before the board deck is due Friday morning**.

Test a job story by asking whether it names a trigger, a constraint, and a
deadline or consequence. "When I want good reporting, I want good reports, so I
can report well" is a tautology, not a job.

### 5. Interview to Saturation, Then Stop
Run interviews in waves of 5, not one large batch, so you can revise the guide
between waves. Saturation is when a wave produces no new problem categories —
typically 8 to 15 people within a single well-defined segment.

Track it concretely: keep a running list of distinct problems named. If
interviews 11 through 15 add only variations of things already on the list, you
have saturated that segment. Crossing a segment boundary — different role,
company size, or industry — resets the count; five more admins does not tell you
anything about finance leads.

### 6. Close With the Commitment Question
End every interview by asking for something that costs the person a little:
an intro to a colleague, a follow-up session with their real data, a pilot slot,
or a deposit. Enthusiasm that evaporates the moment you ask for 30 more minutes
was compliment, not signal.

## Warning Signs

- Every interview ends with the interviewee liking the idea
- Questions that describe your solution before the person describes their problem
- Findings phrased as "users want" with no story or date behind them
- Recruiting only from your existing happy customers or your own network
- One interviewer who also owns the roadmap, taking their own notes
- Counting how many people said yes, rather than what they had already tried
- Declaring saturation after four conversations, or after mixing three segments
- Personas with names, ages, and stock photos but no observed behaviour
